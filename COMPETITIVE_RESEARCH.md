# Competitive research notes

## What the strongest wine apps do well

Vivino is strongest at fast label scanning, huge community ratings, taste match / personalization, shopping, wishlist, and approachable wine discovery. Its UX feels premium when it makes a bottle instantly legible: rating, price, taste profile, food match, and whether it suits you.

CellarTracker wins with serious cellar management: inventory, storage locations, deliveries, consumption history, professional/community reviews, cellar value, drinking windows, and large-scale collector workflows. Its depth is the product.

Delectable is strongest as a wine diary / social feed: photograph bottle, quick rating/note, follow sommeliers/winemakers/critics, private or shared posts, and social discovery. Premium feel comes from being quick and conversational, closer to Instagram for wine than a database.

Wine-Searcher is the price and availability tool: vintage/producer/region knowledge, price comparison, merchant lookup, local store discovery, wishlist/saved wines, and value validation.

Hello Vino is strongest at guided choice: food pairings, occasions, taste preference Q&A, approachable recommendations, wine guide/dictionary, and a no-snob framing.

## What makes UX feel premium

Fast capture, minimal typing, clean hierarchy around the bottle, good photography, tasteful typography, subtle motion/haptics, and turning raw cellar data into guidance: drink now, pair with this, buy again, value, and taste patterns.

Premium wine UX is calm, not gamified. It should feel like a private tasting journal with a sommelier whispering useful context, not a shopping app yelling offers.

## Common complaints

Vivino users complain about ads, aggressive premium gates, shopping taking over the product, confusing redesigns, buried search/community, inability to edit some history/location records, weak cellar filters, and marketplace/customer-service issues.

CellarTracker users value depth but complain about mobile complexity, too many drilldowns for location/bin workflows, awkward one-handed UX, and some missing professional-review visibility in newer flows.

Delectable complaints include label recognition misses, slow app/database gaps, limited organization/search for saved wines, and poor purchase availability.

Wine-Searcher complaints include only okay label recognition, forced saved label photos, confusing saved/wishlist behavior, merchant trust/quality concerns, and missing recommendations by style/price/location.

Hello Vino complaints include paywalls for scanner, scan reliability, loading/account bugs, and inconsistent purchase links.

## Opportunities for Cellar Native

Because Cellar is local-first, it should avoid the biggest category sins: no ads, no marketplace pressure, no forced account, no hostile premium gates, no noisy social feed. It can still borrow the best ideas: guided food pairing, drinking windows, cellar value, wishlist/purchase history, sommelier insights, beautiful taste/value visualizations, and lightweight share cards.

Best local-first features to add now: auto sommelier guidance from entered fields, drink-window recommendations, pairing suggestions, cellar value summary, purchase/value tracking, wishlist/readiness filters, and a shareable tasting note. Keep all suggestions transparent and editable rather than pretending to have a global wine database.
